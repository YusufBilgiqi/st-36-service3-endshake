01 ‚Äî Definition

What Is End Shake?

End shake is the small amount of axial (up-and-down) freedom a wheel arbor has inside its jewel bearings. It is the direct result of the spacing between the top and bottom jewels ‚Äî the gap between their faces is intentionally slightly larger than the length of the pivot.

This tiny movement ‚Äî fractions of a millimetre ‚Äî is not an accident or a manufacturing tolerance to be minimised. It is a deliberate design requirement that every wheel in the movement depends on.

Too Little End Shake
The pivot binds against the jewel surfaces. Power is lost to friction at every revolution. In severe cases the wheel simply stops turning ‚Äî the movement halts. Even slight binding that doesn't stop the train will rob amplitude from the escapement, making the watch an unreliable timekeeper.
Too Much End Shake
The arbor can tip and wobble. Wheel teeth move out of their correct mesh plane with the pinion, causing uneven tooth engagement and erratic power delivery. In extreme cases, teeth can ride over pinion leaves rather than meshing cleanly ‚Äî and the escapement geometry changes as the balance tilts in its pivot holes.

02 ‚Äî The Gradient Rule

03 ‚Äî The Exception

Pallet Fork & Balance ‚Äî Must Match

Every rule has an exception. While end shake decreases from barrel to escape wheel, the pallet fork and balance wheel are a special case: their end shakes should be visually the same as each other.

The reason is escapement geometry. The divisions (clearances) between the pallet fork and the impulse roller/safety roller must remain consistent whether the movement is dial-up or dial-down. If the pallet fork has more end shake than the balance wheel, it shifts further away from the balance in one position ‚Äî the clearances that were correct dial-up become wrong when flipped. This causes escapement faults that only appear in vertical positions: the guard pin rubs the safety roller, or the impulse pin misses the fork notch cleanly. Equal end shakes keep the geometry stable across both horizontal positions.

05 ‚Äî The Skill

Judging End Shake Visually

There is no precision instrument for measuring end shake in a working movement. It is assessed entirely by sight ‚Äî specifically by watching how a pivot moves inside its jewel hole under magnification while you gently lift the wheel with a clean oiler.

The technique: With the movement in the holder, use the tip of a clean, dry oiler to gently lift each arbor from below. Watch the pivot through your loupe. The pivot will rise slightly, then settle back under its own weight when you release. The visual distance it travels ‚Äî how much it appears to move inside the jewel hole ‚Äî is your read of the end shake.

What You're Watching
As you lift the arbor, the pivot visibly rises inside the jewel hole. When you release, it drops back. The vertical travel you can see inside the hole is the end shake. With experience you develop a reference memory ‚Äî you know what "feels right" because you've seen a movement that runs well. Healthy end shake looks springy and free; no end shake looks stuck; too much looks floppy and vague.
The Gradient in Practice
Check each wheel in sequence: barrel ‚Üí centre ‚Üí 3rd ‚Üí 4th ‚Üí escape. Each successive wheel should visibly have less axial travel than the one before it. If you see the 3rd wheel moving as freely as the barrel ‚Äî or the escape wheel moving as much as the centre wheel ‚Äî that's a sign of a problem: a worn jewel hole, a damaged bridge, or wrong components fitted together.
Also check with the movement upside down. Turn it over and repeat the oiler test. Each arbor should still drop under its own weight. If an arbor fails to fall in one orientation, it indicates a bent pivot, a dirty jewel hole, or a bridge that's too tight on the plate. Identifying this before oiling saves having to clean everything again.
06 ‚Äî The Backspin Test

The Real Confirmation ‚Äî Back-Spin

Visual end shake assessment tells you about individual wheel freedom. The back-spin test tells you how well all the wheels work together ‚Äî and it's this combined feel that watchmakers rely on most.

What the Back-Spin Test Reveals

Correct ‚Äî Smooth Recoil

With a few turns of mainspring power, the escape wheel is unlocked with pegwood. The train runs briefly, then the escape wheel stops and recoils smoothly, locking gently on the pallet. The back-spin is fluid and quiet ‚Äî the whole train "breathes" together.

Too Little End Shake

The train feels stiff or sluggish. Under dial-down or in vertical positions, a wheel may bind intermittently. Amplitude drops noticeably between positions. The back-spin may hesitate before stopping cleanly.

‚ùå

Too Much End Shake

The train runs but sounds loose or uneven. Wheels may wobble perceptibly on their arbors. If the back-spin recoil is erratic ‚Äî different from tooth to tooth on the escape wheel ‚Äî excessive end shake may be the cause of unstable locking.

The Upside-Down Check

Run the back-spin test in both orientations. The train should run equally freely dial-up and dial-down. A significant difference points to a pivot or jewel problem on the lower side. This is especially important for the balance and pallet fork pair.

The goal is replication, not measurement. When you've serviced a movement and it runs beautifully, study how the back-spin feels ‚Äî how each wheel's end shake looks under your loupe. You're building a physical memory of a working train. Future movements are assessed against that memory, not against a chart of numbers.

Key Principles ‚Äî Summary

What It Is

Axial freedom of a pivot between its two jewel bearings. A deliberate, necessary clearance ‚Äî not a flaw.

The Gradient

Decreases from barrel to escape wheel. Each wheel has visibly less end shake than the one before it.

The Exception

Pallet fork and balance wheel must have equal end shakes ‚Äî escapement geometry depends on it across both horizontal positions.

Too Little

Pivot binds on jewel. Power loss, low amplitude, potential stoppage. More common in worn or badly assembled movements.

Too Much

Wheel tilts, teeth skip mesh plane. In escapement: clearances and locking geometry become position-dependent.

How to Judge

10√ó magnification minimum. Lift each arbor with a clean oiler ‚Äî watch pivot travel. Then back-spin the train to confirm everything works together.
